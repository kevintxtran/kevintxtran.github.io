ResMed

UX DESIGN INTERNSHIP | SUMMER 2018

Introduction

This past summer, I’ve had the pleasure of being a UX Intern at ResMed. ResMed is a medical equipment based company whose speciality lies in developing technology to treat sleep apnea. This includes but is not limited to portable oxygen concentrators (POCs), continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) machines, and masks. I was brought on to work on the various software that would allow patients to visualize their sleep data as well as other internal software.

Goals

I wanted real experience working with people. Being a student, it’s easy to forget that you spend most of your time in a bubble, among like-minded and like-aged people. This internship gave me the opportunity to work with people of varying ages and backgrounds from around the world. I wanted to understand people through a much larger scope.

My primary goal was to learn the ins and outs of the user research process and how to optimally extract information through user testing.

My secondary goal was to improve my technical design skills, more specifically, creating wireframes and user flows.

What I worked on

I was tasked with working on a variety of projects, allowing me to hone many different areas of UX research and design. These tasks included

  • Moderated user testing
  • Unmoderated user testing
  • Creating testing protocol
  • Creating wireframes and lo-fi prototypes
  • and more.

My first week, I was greeted with a lot of confusing ResMed software that all sounded alike to me.

  • myAir
  • myMobi
  • AirMini
  • AirView
  • AirSense
  • Airbnb

Just kidding about the last one.

But there was one project that allowed me to really test my design skills: the revamp of an internal app, ResAssist. ResAssist is an app that would only be used by ResMed employees. There was already an iteration of the ResAssist app, but it needed a huge redesign. The app was created with WordPress.

I didn’t even know you could create apps with WordPress.

ResAssist

There was no clear direction as to what a ResMedian would do with ResAssist. Therefore my team, the Global Health Informatics UX Team, reached out to the IT department for additional debriefing.

Disclaimer: I am unable to share any prototypes. I apologize!

The ResAssist app

To try to understand what was wrong with the app, I performed guerrilla testing on 10 employees. Every app begins with a purpose, or else it becomes irrelevant. Therefore, given that the purpose of ResAssist is to "facilitate the life of a daily ResMed employee," how well did employees think it fulfilled its purpose?

The Problem

Users see no value in ResAssist. They have no reason to open the app for whatever reason. If users saw no value in ResAssist, how could we hope to create value?

Wireframing and More Testing

Users had no interest in the old ResAssist because it was poorly designed. The new ResAssist aims to fix the usability problems and provide a fresh look to ResMed employees. After the first round of usability testing, I created wireframes for the first iteration of a redesigned ResAssist.

The wireframes included

  • An events page to allow ResMed employees to keep up all the internal events
  • A support page to allow easy access to technical support
  • AJAIJ An integrated ticket system, allowing employees to forego an unpleasant web experience

Another round of guerrilla testing ensued. 10 users were tested -- 5 who I tested in the first round, and 5 completely new users. Analyzing a change in reception was important to me because it allowed me to develop even more rapport. However, I understood how biased testing the same users would be, thus I looked to test on 5 new users.

More testing

Our ticketing idea... did not come to fruition. Unfortunately, it was at this point of the project where deadlines and feasibility issues came to fruition instead.

Therefore, our primary focus was visually updating ResAssist as well as continuing to conduct more user testing. The infeasibility of ServiceNow integration resulted in the creation of a dashboard – a homepage that contains information relevant to the ResMed employee. This dashboard consisted of a lunch menu, a list of recommended/saved events, and floor plans (people are moving all the time. This dashboard update was paired with updates to color and iconography.

ResAssist v3 -- a visual kick

After another set of user testing, our findings started to show some consistency across different employees.

But the most prominent finding? ResMed employees like getting work done. It's their favorite part of the day, not lunch! When they sit in meetings and at their desks all day, it’s unfulfilling and frustrating to not make any progress. With the variety of software available to employees, a bad user experience paired with unproductive meetings can easily snowball into a poor day.

With this finding, the possibilities seemed endless. How could we help ResMed employees feel accomplished? This question intrigued me to no end.

What did I get out of this project?

Working with Axure. I’m very adamant about wireframing with Sketch because it looked the best out of other software I’ve used (wireframes need to look somewhat pretty too!) But I’ve never had more fun creating wireframes. Axure was easy and intuitive, yet powerful when it came to creating wireframes and user flows.

Really evaluating user flows and information architecture. I was able to articulate my evaluations much clearer. I improved my ability to critique rather than criticize.

Working with a variety of people. As I’ve mentioned before, all of my previous projects have involved college students between the ages of 18-21. I like to think I can guess what their responses will be. In this environment, I had absolutely no clue what anyone was thinking, which is the beauty of user testing. I loved not knowing.
