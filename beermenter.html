Beermenter

UX DESIGN PROJECT | FALL 2018

Role: UX Researcher, UX Designer, Visual Designer

Tools: Axure, Sketch

Skills: Research, Wireframing, Visual Design

Introduction

With over 300 thousand different kinds of beers today, beer has created a community of zythophiles. Beer culture is rather prevalent in the U.S. to where even my campus, UC San Diego, holds an event called Bear [Beer] Garden where different beers are offered for those of age to explore. The Beermenter project was brought up with the intention to tackle any problems one would encounter when consuming beer. In the class COGS 120: Interaction Design, I worked with a team of 3 to discover and tackle any possible problems one may come across when drinking beer.

Scope

The inspiration for this project stems from my team’s confusion entering the beer culture. As a team of 3 who have all recently turned 21, we were unfamiliar with the terminology, flavors, and how to even order; it was difficult to choose the “right” beer based solely off the name. My team wanted to design an app that would tackle this issue by “mentoring” newcomers, making their beer-related selections quicker and easier.

Needfinding

Before going out into the field, my team wanted to know the exact number of people we’d be designing for. That is — how many people actually drink beer? And how often do they attend breweries?.

It was important for us to perform an ethnographic study (with participants’ permission ) before asking for a formal interview. We wanted to observe the entire brewery experience from purchasing beers to chugging one down. Once we finished the observation process, we noticed that three questions extracted the richest data from our participants.

  1. Why do you come to breweries?
  2. Why do you drink beer?
  3. How do you know what beer to buy?

Talking to some brewery goers to discover their needs

  1. People tend to attend breweries for the social experience rather than the beer itself. There are many components that are important to a customer at a restaurant — atmosphere, food, etc. But at a brewery, the most important factor was the company.
  2. People drink beer because it has a flavor like no other. Most beers tend to be bitter, but there are so many different flavor profiles that drinking.
  3. Most of the time people don’t know what kind of drinks they want. They’re confronted with the paradox of choice — the idea that the more choices presented to someone, the less likely they are to make a decision. When coming to brewery, a menu can have anywhere from 10 to 50 beers. But those who constantly want to try new flavors are essentially flipping a coin when it comes to selecting a good flavor.

Storyboards

We produced storyboards of what we believed would be the scenario where users may use an app to solve their beer-related problem. These storyboards helped us assign a narrative our findings.

The Problem

Based on our findings, we came to the conclusion that users’ number one issue was that there are just too many beers (and thus flavor profiles) to keep track of.

Wireframes

We created wireframes of what we thought would fulfill our users' needs — an app that would simplify the beer selection process? But how exactly would we do that?

First iteration of wireframes

As the sole designer, I created low-fidelity wireframes as a way to easily visualize the necessary changes to provide my team a visible direction for the app. I designed how a user could pick a beer from start to finish, first beginning with the initial question of which brewery they were at, what kind of taste profile did they desire, and so forth til they reached their suggested beer. Design iteration was faster through sketching, allowing us to change our sketches easily after our initial round of feedback.

Heuristic Evaluation

Despite the critique of our initial prototypes, we knew that our wireframes were far from ready. Therefore, we and others performed a heuristic evaluation on our prototype using Nielsen’s 10 Heuristics. When you get deeper and deeper into the design process, it’s easy to forget who you’re designing for. A heuristic evaluation allowed our team narrow our focus even further and refocus on the user.

Biggest violations included:

Familiar metaphors and language. Users can filter beers through a variety of traits, such as type and flavor. However, these traits, especially for people who are new to the beer drinking scene, can be a bit ambiguous. For example, what is an IPA? What exactly is the flavor of “golden” beer?

User control and freedom. Although users can be recommended beer, there’s no way for users to keep track of what they’ve drank. Users may be recommended the same beer over and over, and there’s nothing the user can do about it.

Other less notable violations include a violation of error prevention and aesthetic/minimalist design.

Visual Design/Prototyping

After all the feedback we received on our wireframes, it was finally time to breathe some life into them through visual design. I created a simple style guide that would be implemented throughout the web app.

For an app that focuses on breweries, I opted to go for a night-scheme. I wanted to choose colors that not only reminded people of beer, but also remind people that beer connects. It is a social catalyst and despite being chilled, evokes a warm feeling when surrounded by company.

Usability Testing

For this round of usability testing, we gave users a lot of freedom to explore the app. Becaues functionality of the app was minimal, we didn't provide specific tasks for the user to follow. However, we did give a general task list for the user as our primary focus was pinpointing usability issues. If an issue was encountered, users were further questioned to determine how severe the usability issue was.

We also created performance metrics to determine if users engaged with a feature or saw it as useful. Some metrics include:

  • how many times a user mentions a filter
  • how many times a user clicks heart
  • how many times a user mentions menu/navigation

Users as they navigate the web prototype of Beermenter

Once we outlined the issues of our app, we sought out to fix those issues. These included but are not limited to poor, unintuitive menu navigation and a plethora of unnecessary features in which users saw no purpose.

Conclusion

Time and time again I learn the sensitive nature of design. It always challenges me by asking me what I’m going to do when things don’t go the way I expect. That’s what I love about design — it’s the perfect field where you should expect the unexpected.

Things I wish I could’ve done:

Make complete use of the login. I would’ve liked to implement a profile and some sort of share functionality. After all, from what we’ve learned from users, visiting breweries is socially driven.

Find better spots to explain the words associated with beer. There is a lot of beer terminology. And despite the fact that users encountered little to no issues when it came to learning what this terminology meant, I can’t help but think that there’s more I could’ve done to immerse themselves in the beer culture. Maybe a tutorial… or maybe I’m overthinking everything.
