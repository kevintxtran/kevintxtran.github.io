Beermenter

Role: UX Designer, Visual Designer

Duration: 10 weeks

Tools: Axure, Sketch

Skills: Research, Wireframing, Visual Design

Introduction

I recently turned 21. With 21 comes a responsibility — drinking alcohol. Though, the segway for many people comes is drinking. There’s a huge beer drinking culture around the US and even an event on the UC San Diego campus called Bear [Beer] Garden. With such a culture surrounding the alcoholic beverage, I wanted to solve any problems people might have while drinking beer.

Scope

As a team of 3 who have all recently turned 21, the world of beer is intimidating. There are so many different types and flavor profiles that it’s intimidating to even order at a brewery. There’s an entire culture surrounding the world of beer, which is why we wanted to create an app that would be beginner friendly. When trying something new, people shouldn’t be intimidated by the number of choices or by words they don’t understand. Our inspiration came from our own confusion as young adults entering a completely new field.

Needfinding

Before going out into the field, my team wanted to know the exact number of people we’d be designing for. That is — how many people actually drink beer? And how often do they attend breweries?.

It was important for us to perform an ethnographic study (with participants’ permission of course) before asking for a formal interview. We wanted to observe the entire brewery experience from purchasing beers to chugging one down. Once we finished the observation process, we noticed that three questions extracted the richest data from our participants.

  1. Why do you come to breweries?
  2. Why do you drink beer?
  3. How do you know what beer to buy?

These were the three most crucial questions when it came our interviews because it revealed their biggest needs.

  1. People tend to attend breweries for the social experience rather than the beer itself. There are many components that are important to a customer at a restaurant — atmosphere, food, etc. But at a brewery, the most important factor was the company.
  2. People drink beer because it has a flavor like no other. Most beers tend to be bitter, but there are so many different flavor profiles that drinking.
  3. Most of the time people don’t know what kind of drinks they want. They’re confronted with the paradox of choice — the idea that the more choices presented to someone, the less likely they are to make a decision. When coming to brewery, a menu can have anywhere from 10 to 50 beers. But those who constantly want to try new flavors are essentially flipping a coin when it comes to selecting a good flavor.

Storyboards

Our group created storyboards to help us visualize our user findings. Storyboards helped us assign a narrative to a problem — especially because this problem is relevant to three 21 year olds in a group.

The Problem

Based on our findings, we came to the conclusion that users’ number one issue was that there are just too many beers (and thus flavor profiles) to keep track of.

Wireframes

We sketched out wireframes of what we thought would fulfill our user needs — an app that would simplify the beer selection process? But how exactly would we do that? We first sketched paper prototypes to perform rapid user testing. And although our paper prototypes did look a higher fidelity than your typical paper prototype, there were still many, many issues with the iteration. Sketching allowed us to quickly and easily draw up another UI during our initial round of feedback.

Early wireframes

After we received feedback, we made the necessary changes and ensued with wireframing. As the primary designer, I created low-fidelity wireframes as a way to visualize the changes. The goal of the wireframes was to map out a loose user journey and in the process, give our team some direction.

Heuristic Evaluation

Despite the initial critique of our paper prototypes, we knew that our wireframes were far from ready. Therefore, we and others performed a heuristic evaluation on our prototype using Nielsen’s 10 Heuristics. When you get deeper and deeper into the design process, it’s easy to forget who you’re designing for. A heuristic evaluation allowed our team narrow our focus even further and refocus on the user.

Biggest violations included

Familiar metaphors and language (This violation dumbfounded me because I always thought this heuristic was the easiest to fulfill). Users can filter beers through a variety of traits, such as type and flavor. However, these traits, especially for people who are new to the beer drinking scene, can be a bit ambiguous. For example, what is an IPA? What exactly is the flavor of “golden” beer?

User control and freedom Although users can be recommended beer, there’s no way for users to keep track of what they’ve drank. Users may be recommended the same beer over and over, and there’s nothing the user can do about it.

Other less notable violations include a violation of error prevention and aesthetic/minimalist design.

For an app that focuses on breweries, I opted to go for a night-scheme. I wanted to choose colors that not only reminded people of beer, but also remind people that beer connects. It is a social catalyst and despite being chilled, evokes a warm feeling when surrounded by company.

Usability Testing

Even with a solid deliverable, our team always kept in mind that there is always room for improvement. For this round of usability testing, we gave users a lot of freedom when exploring the app. We did not provide specific tasks because the functionality of the app minimal (compared to a social media app). However, we did give a general task list for the user as our primary focus was pinpointing usability issues. If an issue was encountered, users were further questioned to determine how severe the usability issue was.

Once we outlined the issues of our app, we sought out to fix those issues. These included but are not limited to poor menu navigation and unnecessary features. Another second round of usability testing was conducted, but with more directed tasks. Our team created performance metrics to determine the superior redesign. Some metrics included

  • how many times a user mentions a filter
  • how many times a user clicks heart
  • how many times a user mentions menu/navigation

Conclusion

Time and time again I learn the sensitive nature of design. It always challenges me by asking me what I’m going to do when things don’t go the way I expect. That’s what I love about design — it’s the perfect field where you should expect the unexpected.

Things I wish I could’ve done:

Make complete use of the login. I would’ve liked to implement a profile and some sort of share functionality. After all, from what we’ve learned from users, visiting breweries is socially driven.

Find better spots to explain the words associated with beer.There is a lot of beer terminology. And despite the fact that users encountered little to no issues when it came to learning what this terminology meant, I can’t help but think that there’s more I could’ve done to immerse themselves in the beer culture. Maybe a tutorial… or maybe I’m overthinking everything.

I’d like to thank Justin and Etsu for doing an amazing job on this project — you guys were both amazing teammates and I’m proud of what were able to do in such a short amount of time.
