Healthy Dining Habits

UX DESIGN PROJECT | WINTER 2018

Role: UX Researcher, UX Designer

Duration: 6 weeks

Tools: Sketch, Invision

Skills: Storyboarding, Rapid User Testing, Wireframing

Introduction

College students suck at eating healthy. Research shows that most don’t even get their recommended one of five servings of vegetables a day. But maybe they’re not entirely to blame -- there aren’t many options for healthy, quick meals on campus. To combat this college epidemic, we designed app rewards system called Healthy Dining Habits.

We hoped to collaborate with UCSD Housing Dining & Hospitality (HDH) so that, based on the number of points they earn from healthy purchases, they’ll be entered in a lottery for prizes such as Amazon gift cards.

Needfinding

First and foremost, we wanted to interview students who lived on campus and learn why they opt for unhealthy foods. As students ourselves, we could hypothesize what our interviewees would answer, but wanted to empathize with other students throughout the interview process.

UC San Diego’s dining system revolves around “dining dollars,” money that can only be spent at campus markets and dining halls. From our interviews, we learned that students do not view dining dollars as real money because it is included in their tuition. This system enables students to sporadically spend their food on what tastes good, rather than what is good for their body. It’s easier to open a bag of chips than to purchase fresh ingredients and cook at home.

Doing field work and observing students' purchases

We had also put out a survey and received 21 responses. Students who lived on campus reported that they valued nutrition over all else. However, our field observations revealed the exact opposite: students prioritize convenience over nutrition. They would stand in front of the frozen food aisles and chips section, contemplating between various unhealthy foods.

Our findings led us to the following question: how might we encourage UCSD students to eat healthier?

Initial Personas

In order to further understand our stakeholders, we created three different personas.

  1. An unhealthy student who doesn’t know how to cook but also wants to eat healthier.
  2. A health-conscious student who has no time to learn new recipes.
  3. An HDH manager who understands that students lack the necessary nutrients.

Our initial three personas

Storyboarding

We as a team created multiple storyboards in order to view our problems within the context of the problem space. During this stage of our ideation, the sky was the limit.

Storyboarding: contextualizing problems

Ideation

At this point of the ideation process, we needed to turn wild ideas into feasible ones. Because our initial personas had revolved the idea that healthy eating should be done through cooking, we thought that pre-prepared meal boxes were a possible solution -- human centered design.

However, this “solution” would be nothing new. Meal prep boxes already exist and students, no matter how easy it may be, do not want to cook on a busy schedule. Therefore, we wanted to design something that would be unique to UC San Diego students.

We came to the conclusion that we didn't fully understand who our users were.

If we couldn't do meal prep boxes, what needed to change? We refined our personas in order to re-contextualize our target users.

Our new, refined personas

College students have busy lives. 24 hours just isn't enough time for them to do everything they want to do -- it becomes so easy to resort to comfort foods. We knew we couldn’t force students to eat healthy. Thus, we sought to incentivize healthy eating through a mobile app instead.

Rapid Prototyping

It hit us. A rewards system! You can lead a horse to water but you can't make it drink.

Okay, maybe that analogy doesn't perfectly fit. What I meant to say is: we can't make students eat healthy, but we can incentivize it through gamification. After all, who doesn't love to compete?

I sketched up some possible mockups of the app on paper. Paper prototyping allowed our team to really take advantage of our design pivot. We went through many iterations, allowing us to pinpoint inconsistencies and fix them in minutes.

It was at this point where I realized: I've never been so excited to create.

Uncut sketches for rapid user testing

Wireframes

Based on what students purchase at the markets, they earn healthy points. Students may view how many points they've earned, as well as a history of their market purchases, on their profile. Once the student exceeds a certain threshold of points, they may enter into a weekly lottery for prizes.

We decided to test our wireframes first to see how well everything flowed. At this stage of the process, we valued functionality and fluidity over all else.

Wireframes of the app. We jumped into high-fidelity mockups and tested.

Initial Mockups & Changes

We created our high fidelity prototypes based on our test findings. Users had little to no problems with the flow of the wireframes. However, once we got to testing our high fidelity prototypes, there were some changes that needed to be made.

Rewards Process

Changes: More screens for the raffle procress, a larger progress bar, and a slide interaction.
Why: Users noted that entering the raffle felt "off" and felt as if it needed more pizazz. The screen also is filled to the brim with information and needed a touching up. A larger progress bar would help the user own their progress and be proud of their accomplishment. Entering a raffle needed more significance because it was the culmination of obtaining enough points.

Profile Page

Changes: We decreased the visibility of the date/time and placed more of an emphasis on points. We added points earned from each item (with color coding) along with a point total from each purchase.
Why: Users felt like the prioritization of information was incorrect; they wanted information concerning their points and how many points an item was worth.

Raffle Status

Changes: Users can see the status of their raffle entry on the home screen, replacing the greeting message.
Why: Users didn't want to dive into the Rewards tab just to check if they had entered the raffle. A greeting message, although encouraging, becomes redundant after a set number of logins.

Final Mockup

Reflection

I couldn't be more proud of our team, to realize our errors and refocus with such short deadlines. One of my issues as a designer is fixating on a solution -- but we successfully pivoted from our narrowminded thinking and delivered a solution we believed to be useful to the students of UCSD.

A major takeaway from this entire design process was learning what it means to take part in a user-centered design. My team’s initial approach to this problem space was to encourage healthy eating by placing boxes in an aisle that students never visited in the first place. What we thought was a good solution might have worked out, but it was not the correct solution. We as a team needed to analyze our findings even further and truly understand what the user needs.

Empathy is a compelling emotion. As a student researching students and designing for them, it puts into perspective how similar some of our problems really are. We all have exams and projects to worry about, so sometimes we place our health to the bottom of the priority list. Being able to to empathize with these students through storyboarding and interviewing contributed to this gratifying design process.
